- **Include**: The points a strong answer should cover. These can also be used as scoring criteria.
- **Avoid**: Common traps or weak framing to stay away from.
- **Standard Answer**: A polished sample answer in Hao's voice. Adapt the length based on interview context.

- **Leadership**: Hybrid IC and design leader with 6 years of direct people-management experience, 3 years managing cross-functional teams across interaction, visual, and 3D design, internal instructor experience, final-round design interviewer authorization, and strong promotion outcomes for eligible team members.

## 3. Portfolio Review / Case Study Review

The default project for this section should usually be the **Baidu App homepage redesign**, because it combines product strategy, UX craft, experimentation, platform scale, and business impact. Use **Duxiaoxiao** when the interviewer is focused on AI, emerging interaction, 0-to-1 product work, or design leadership.

### 1. Walk us through a project you are proud of.

**Include**
- Choose one flagship case and state why it matters.
- Use a clear narrative: context, problem, role, process, trade-offs, outcome, learning.
- For general roles, lead with Baidu App homepage redesign.

**Avoid**
- Walking through every screen chronologically.
- Starting with visuals before product context.
- Taking more than 8-10 minutes without checkpoints.

**Standard Answer**

One project I am proud of is the Baidu App homepage redesign, especially the 2019 version. Baidu App was a very high-scale product with about 200 million daily active users, and the homepage was the most important entry point for search, content discovery, user engagement, and monetization. The core challenge was that the market had shifted toward algorithmic feeds and time-spent competition, while Baidu App still carried a strong search-first homepage structure. My role as design architect was to help move the homepage toward a search-and-algorithmic-feed model without breaking the existing search mental model. We explored multiple hierarchy options, aligned stakeholders, validated through A/B and reversal experiments, and gradually moved the product toward a cleaner and more engagement-oriented structure. The redesign improved key engagement metrics, and the data science team estimated roughly RMB 100 million in annualized revenue growth.

### 2. What was the problem you were solving?

**Include**
- State the user, business, and product-architecture problem.
- Explain market shift from active search to content discovery.
- Mention preserving search clarity while increasing feed prominence.

**Avoid**
- Saying the problem was just "the homepage looked old."
- Focusing only on business metrics.
- Ignoring user mental model.

**Standard Answer**

The problem was not simply visual redesign. Baidu App needed to evolve its homepage product model. Users still needed fast access to search, but mobile behavior had shifted toward continuous content discovery through algorithmic feeds. From the business side, engagement, time spent, and downstream conversion had become increasingly important. So the design problem was how to give the algorithmic feed a more central role while preserving the clarity and trust of search, and how to do that safely on a product with hundreds of millions of users.

I also managed designers on the project, but I stayed hands-on in the most important product and interaction decisions. Because the homepage affected many business teams, a major part of my role was making the design rationale clear enough for stakeholders to understand what we were optimizing for and what risks we were managing.

### 5. What constraints did you have to work within?

**Avoid**

We also had to respect the existing user mental model around search. If we pushed the feed too aggressively, we could damage clarity and trust. There were also business constraints because many teams depended on homepage traffic, and technical constraints around implementation and experiment rollout. That is why we used phased exploration, limited-traffic experiments, and reversal experiments rather than making one large unvalidated change.

### 6. What were the user needs behind the project?

## 4. Design Exercise / Whiteboard / App Critique

### 1. How would you approach redesigning this product flow?

**Include**
- Clarify goal, user, context, current friction, constraints, success metrics.

- Prioritize and test.

**Avoid**
- Jumping directly into UI changes.
- Critiquing without understanding business goals.
- Designing for yourself instead of target users.

**Standard Answer**

I would start by clarifying the goal of the flow, the primary user segment, the business objective, and any technical or organizational constraints. Then I would map the current journey and identify where users lose clarity, confidence, or motivation. After that, I would prioritize the biggest friction based on user impact and business impact, sketch a few alternative directions, and define how we would test them. My goal would be to improve the flow in a way that is easier for users, measurable for the business, and realistic for the team to ship.

### 2. What problem would you solve first?

**Include**
- Prioritize based on user impact, business impact, evidence, and feasibility.
- Identify the bottleneck, not the most visible flaw.
- Explain your reasoning.

**Avoid**
- Picking a surface UI issue without analysis.
- Saying "everything needs redesign."
- Ignoring constraints.

**Standard Answer**

I would solve the problem that creates the biggest user drop-off or misunderstanding while also affecting the product goal. I usually look for the bottleneck: where users hesitate, make errors, abandon the flow, or fail to understand value. If the data shows a clear drop-off, I would start there. If data is not available, I would form a hypothesis based on the journey, then validate it quickly through usability testing or a lightweight experiment. I would not start with visual polish unless the visual issue is directly causing comprehension or trust problems.

### 3. What assumptions would you want to validate?

**Include**
- User intent, comprehension, trust, motivation, information needs, technical feasibility.
- Include business assumptions and metric assumptions.
- Mention fastest validation method.

**Avoid**
- Validating only UI preference.
- Asking overly broad research questions.
- Forgetting feasibility assumptions.

**Standard Answer**

I would validate several assumptions. First, what is the user's real intent when they enter the flow? Second, do they understand the value proposition and the next step? Third, what information do they need before they feel comfortable continuing? Fourth, where are we assuming trust that users may not actually have? I would also validate business assumptions, such as whether the proposed change is likely to improve conversion or retention, and technical assumptions around what can be shipped. Depending on risk, I would use usability testing, prototype testing, analytics review, or a small experiment.

### 4. Where do you see the biggest friction?

- Tie friction to user behavior.
- Offer evidence you would seek.

**Avoid**
- Saying "the design is ugly."
- Making unsupported claims too confidently.
- Listing many issues without priority.

**Standard Answer**

I look for friction in a few places: whether users understand what the product is asking them to do, whether the next step is visually and conceptually clear, whether the information hierarchy supports decision-making, and whether users receive feedback after taking action. I also look for trust friction, especially if the product asks for personal information, payment, or reliance on AI output. The biggest friction is usually the place where user uncertainty and product importance overlap. I would confirm that through analytics, session observation, or usability testing.

### 5. How would you simplify this experience?

**Include**
- Reduce decision load, clarify IA, remove redundant steps, prioritize key information.
- Preserve necessary complexity.
- Mention progressive disclosure.

**Avoid**
- Equating simplicity with fewer features only.
- Hiding critical information.
- Removing business-critical steps without understanding why they exist.

**Standard Answer**

I would simplify the experience by first identifying what decision the user needs to make at each step. Then I would remove or defer information that does not support that decision. I would also look for repeated choices, unclear labels, unnecessary navigation, and places where the system could provide smarter defaults. But I would not remove complexity blindly. In many products, some complexity is necessary for trust or compliance, so the goal is to make complexity understandable through better hierarchy, grouping, progressive disclosure, and clearer feedback.

### 6. What information should be more prominent?

**Include**
- Prioritize information tied to user decision-making.
- Consider context, timing, and intent.
- Mention visual hierarchy and scanning.

**Avoid**
- Making everything prominent.
- Prioritizing business messages over user needs without justification.
- Treating prominence as only font size.

**Standard Answer**

The most prominent information should be whatever helps the user make the next important decision. That usually includes the value of the action, the current state, the primary next step, and any trust or risk information needed to continue. I would use hierarchy, grouping, spacing, contrast, and interaction states to make that information easier to scan. A common mistake is making too many elements compete for attention. Good hierarchy should make the primary path obvious while still keeping secondary information available.

### 7. What metrics would you use to evaluate your solution?

**Include**
- Use primary metric plus guardrail metrics.
- Mention task completion, conversion, retention, time, errors, satisfaction, trust.
- Tailor to the product.

**Avoid**

- Optimizing one metric only.

**Standard Answer**

I would define one or two primary metrics based on the product goal, such as task completion, conversion, activation, engagement, or retention. Then I would add guardrail metrics to make sure the solution does not create harm, such as error rate, support tickets, time to complete, user satisfaction, trust perception, or long-term retention. For high-scale products, I would also look at segment differences because an improvement for one user group may not help another. My goal is to connect metrics to the actual user behavior we want to improve.

### 8. How would you test this design before launch?

**Include**
- Prototype testing, usability testing, internal review, technical feasibility, A/B testing if possible.
- Match validation depth to risk.
- Mention staged rollout.

**Avoid**

**Standard Answer**

I would choose the testing method based on risk. For an early concept, I would use a prototype to test comprehension, task flow, and user expectations. If the design has complex interaction, I would create a high-fidelity prototype because static screens may not reveal the real experience. Before launch, I would review technical feasibility with engineering and define success metrics with product and data partners. For a high-traffic or business-critical flow, I would prefer a staged rollout or A/B test so we can measure impact and catch issues before full release.

### 9. How would your solution change if engineering capacity were limited?

**Include**

**Standard Answer**

If engineering capacity were limited, I would separate the ideal experience from the smallest useful version. I would work with engineering to understand which parts are expensive and which changes are relatively easy. Then I would prioritize improvements that reduce the biggest user friction with the least implementation risk, such as copy, hierarchy, defaults, layout, or reusing existing components. I would still document the longer-term direction, but the first release should protect the core user value and create a path for future iteration.

### 10. How would you improve trust in this AI feature?

**Include**
- Explain AI capability boundaries, confidence, source/citation, state feedback, user control, privacy.

**Avoid**

Users need to understand what the AI is doing, whether it is answering, searching, generating, or asking for clarification. From my Duxiaoxiao work, I learned that emotionally expressive interaction can make AI feel more approachable, but trust still depends on usefulness, transparency, control, and consistent behavior.
